# Elevation Basics for Contractors

## A Plain-Language Guide to FFE, BFE, NAVD88, and Why It Matters

This guide explains the elevation concepts you'll encounter on a Formwork Elevation Reference Exhibit. No surveying background required.

---

## The Three Numbers You Need to Know

### 1. FFE — Finished Floor Elevation

**What it is:** The elevation of the top surface of your finished slab, measured in feet above sea level.

**Example:** FFE = 621.25' means the top of your finished slab needs to be 621.25 feet above sea level.

**Why it matters:** This is the number the building department checks. If your slab is too low, you fail inspection. If you're in a flood zone, a low slab means the building can flood — and the owner can't get flood insurance.

### 2. BFE — Base Flood Elevation

**What it is:** The elevation that floodwater is expected to reach during a "100-year flood" (a flood that has a 1% chance of happening in any given year).

**Example:** BFE = 620.0' means FEMA predicts floodwater could reach 620 feet above sea level at this location.

**Why it matters:** Building codes require the FFE to be at or above the BFE (and usually above it by a "freeboard" amount — often 1 foot). So if BFE = 620.0' and the jurisdiction requires 1' of freeboard, FFE must be at least 621.0'.

### 3. NAVD88 — North American Vertical Datum of 1988

**What it is:** The reference system that defines what "above sea level" means. It's the ruler that all elevations are measured against.

**Why it matters:** When the exhibit says "621.25' NAVD88," it means 621.25 feet above the NAVD88 reference. This is a nationally standardized system — an elevation of 621.25' NAVD88 in Texas means the same thing as 621.25' NAVD88 in Florida. It's how we compare elevations across the country.

**You don't need to understand the math behind NAVD88.** Just know that all the elevations on your exhibit use this system, and they're all speaking the same language.

## How These Numbers Connect

```
BFE (from FEMA) = 620.0'
      +
Freeboard (from local code) = 1.0'
      =
Minimum FFE = 621.0'
      
Your exhibit specifies FFE = 621.25' (with a small safety margin)
```

Your job: set your forms so the top of the finished slab is at 621.25' or higher.

## What's a Benchmark?

A **benchmark** is a physical point on the job site with a known elevation. It's the thing you measure from.

Think of it this way: you can't set your forms to "621.25 feet above sea level" by looking at a number. You need a physical reference point — something you can put a tape measure on — that the engineer has already tied to the NAVD88 system.

That's what the benchmarks on your exhibit are. The engineer went to the site (or used GPS/GIS data), established the elevation of specific physical points, and documented them.

**Common benchmarks:**
- A screw or nail the engineer installed in the ground
- An existing concrete pad or curb
- An electrical post base
- A fire hydrant flange

Your exhibit tells you which benchmarks to use, where to find them (with photos), and what their elevation is.

---

## The Offset — Bridging the Gap

Here's the key concept: the **offset** is the vertical distance between a benchmark and your required FFE.

**Example:**
- Benchmark (Screw B) elevation = 621.00'
- Required FFE = 621.25'
- Offset = 621.25 - 621.00 = 0.25 feet = 3 inches

That means: if you measure 3 inches above Screw B, you're at the required FFE.

The TideMark stake makes this easy — you set the collar to 3 inches above zero, and that's your target.

## Flood Zones — Quick Reference

Your exhibit includes a FEMA map showing the flood zone. Here's what the common designations mean:

| Zone | What It Means | FFE Required? |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Zone X (unshaded) | Minimal flood risk | Usually no |
| Zone X (shaded) | Moderate flood risk (500-year flood area) | Depends on jurisdiction |
| Zone A | High risk, no published BFE | Yes — community determines BFE |
| Zone AE | High risk, BFE is published | Yes — per BFE + freeboard |
| Zone VE | Coastal high hazard (wave action) | Yes — stricter requirements |

If your project is in Zone A or AE, the FFE requirement is driven by the BFE plus any freeboard the local jurisdiction requires.

---

## Tolerance — How Close Is Close Enough?

The exhibit specifies an elevation tolerance — typically **±0.05 feet** (about 5/8 of an inch).

That means if the required FFE is 621.25', your slab can be anywhere from 621.20' to 621.30' and be within tolerance.

In practice: aim for the specified elevation or slightly above. It's always safer to be a little high than a little low. A slab that's 1/4" too high passes. A slab that's 1/4" too low might not.

## Common Questions

**Q: Why can't I just use a laser level?**
A: A laser level tells you that two points are at the same height relative to each other. It doesn't tell you the absolute elevation (height above sea level). You need at least one point with a known NAVD88 elevation — that's your benchmark. From there, a laser level is a great tool for transferring that elevation across the site.

**Q: What if the ground has been graded since the exhibit was prepared?**
A: The benchmarks may no longer be valid if grading has changed the area around them. Call the engineer before proceeding. This is specifically called out in the exhibit's general notes.

**Q: Do I need a surveyor?**
A: The FERE is designed to be used without a surveyor on site. However, if the offset is very large, the site is complex, or you're uncomfortable with the measurements, a surveyor can verify independently. The exhibit is a reference tool — not a replacement for professional survey services if the situation warrants them.

**Q: What's the difference between this exhibit and an Elevation Certificate?**
A: An Elevation Certificate (FEMA Form 086-0-33) is a post-construction document completed by a licensed surveyor that certifies the actual as-built elevation of a structure. The FERE is a pre-construction reference tool that helps you hit the right elevation before you pour. Different documents, different purposes, different times in the project.
