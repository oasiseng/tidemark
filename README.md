TideMark is a field-friendly reference system for helping contractors and builders set and verify minimum finished floor elevations (FFE) before concrete placement.

The core idea is simple: make it easier to translate elevation requirements into something visible, practical, and repeatable in the field.
TideMark is being developed as a hardware-assisted workflow for slab layout, formwork elevation reference, and construction photo verification. The long-term goal is to create a simple, durable, and contractor-friendly system that reduces confusion around elevation targets while supporting workflows such as FEMA elevation certificate coordination and pre-pour checks.
---
The Problem
In the field, elevation requirements are often known in theory but hard to execute clearly.
Common issues include:
Contractors know the required minimum floor elevation, but do not have a simple field reference to work from
Layout points such as nails, screws, stakes, and stringlines are often temporary, disturbed, or hard to interpret later
Many crews rely on improvised methods like wood stakes, tape measures, and verbal instructions
Photos taken for verification are inconsistent and often not clear enough for documentation
There is a gap between engineering intent, field execution, and permit or FEMA-related documentation
In short, there is often no clean, repeatable way to communicate:
where the current ground or reference point is
where the slab or formwork needs to be
how to document that clearly before the pour
---
The Solution
TideMark aims to solve this by creating a simple reference tool and workflow that can be used in the field.
At a high level, TideMark is intended to:
provide a visible elevation reference near the slab or formwork area
support stringline layout and relative height control
give builders a practical visual target for minimum formwork elevation
make it easier to photograph and verify top-of-form conditions
support communication between contractor, engineer, inspector, and client
The physical product may evolve over time, but the mission is straightforward:
> Turn abstract elevation requirements into clear, field-usable reference points.
